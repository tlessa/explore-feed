Quantum Physics

1/4

Quantum physics describes nature at the smallest scales of atoms and subatomic particles. It reveals bizarre phenomena like wave-particle duality, superposition, and quantum entanglement—what Einstein called "spooky action at a distance."

Unlike classical physics, quantum mechanics shows that particles can exist in multiple states simultaneously until observed. This principle, known as superposition, forms the basis of quantum computing.

The uncertainty principle, formulated by Werner Heisenberg, states that we cannot simultaneously know both the exact position and momentum of a particle. This isn't a limitation of our instruments—it's a fundamental property of nature.

Because you follow Science

Black Holes

2/4

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing—not even light or other electromagnetic waves—can escape once past the event horizon. These cosmic phenomena represent some of the most extreme and mysterious objects in our universe.

Black holes form when massive stars collapse at the end of their life cycle. The boundary around a black hole, called the event horizon, marks the point of no return for anything that crosses it.

At the center lies the singularity, a point of infinite density where our understanding of physics breaks down. Stephen Hawking discovered that black holes emit radiation and slowly evaporate over time.

In 2019, the Event Horizon Telescope captured the first image of a black hole in galaxy M87, confirming Einstein's predictions about these cosmic phenomena.

Because you follow Science

Evolution

3/4

Evolution is the change in heritable characteristics of biological populations over successive generations. Charles Darwin's theory of natural selection, published in 1859 in "On the Origin of Species," revolutionized our understanding of life on Earth.

Natural selection operates on the principle that organisms with traits better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce. Over millions of years, this process has given rise to the incredible diversity of life we see today.

The fossil record, comparative anatomy, and DNA evidence all provide overwhelming support for evolution. Modern genetics has revealed how mutations create variation, while natural selection and genetic drift shape populations over time.

Human evolution traces back approximately 6 million years to our common ancestor with chimpanzees. The discovery of fossils like Lucy (Australopithecus afarensis) and recent DNA analysis have illuminated our species' remarkable journey through Africa and across the globe.
